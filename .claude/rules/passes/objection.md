# Objection Pass Rules

## Scope: What This Pass Does

- Strengthen counterargument handling in the essay — ensure objections are stated fairly and not straw-manned
- Tighten the author's rebuttal to each objection so the response clearly addresses the objection raised
- Flag missing counterarguments that an informed reader would expect to see addressed (but do not write them — flag for author)
- Ensure each counterargument is presented with the same rigor as the author's main argument

## DO NOT Touch (Out of Scope)

- The thesis and main claims — counterargument handling should support and clarify the main position, not change it
- Section structure — locked after the structure pass
- Evidence and citations used in the main argument — do not touch evidence blocks outside the counterargument sections
- The author's argumentative position — this pass may clarify but must not shift the position, even subtly

## Voice Preservation

- Counterarguments should be presented in the same register as the main argument
- Do not make objections sound weaker or less credible by changing their register (e.g., making a serious counterargument sound dismissive)
- The author's characteristic way of handling dissent (acknowledgment + rebuttal, concession + qualification) should be preserved as a rhetorical pattern
- Read `diagnosis.md` author voice patterns before any rewrite — if the author's mode is "anticipate and preempt", do not convert it to "acknowledge and dismiss"

## Factual Integrity

- Do not invent counterarguments the author did not consider — flag missing objections, do not write them
- The correct action for a missing counterargument: "Reader would likely expect a response to [objection X] — not present in draft; requires author input"
- Do not invent responses to objections the author did not address — flag the gap
- Do not alter the strength of a counterargument by changing the evidence it cites; only tighten how the existing objection is presented
