Functionalism is the view that mental states are defined by their functional roles — by what they do, not what they are made of. A belief that it is raining, for instance, is whatever internal state is caused by perceiving rain, causes one to grab an umbrella, and interacts with the desire to stay dry to produce the behavior of going inside. On this view, what matters is the pattern of inputs, outputs, and relations to other mental states, not the physical material that implements it.

This paper argues that functionalism provides the most plausible account of the mind-body relationship. The central argument is as follows. First, mental states are multiply realizable: the same mental state (such as pain) can be implemented in different physical substrates. Humans, octopuses, and hypothetical silicon-based aliens could all experience pain despite having radically different physical constitutions. Second, if mental states can be realized in different physical materials, then mental states cannot be identical to any specific physical state. This rules out the identity theory, which claims that each type of mental state is identical to a specific type of brain state. Third, what all pain-realizers have in common is their functional role: they are caused by tissue damage, they cause distress and avoidance behavior, and they interact with beliefs and desires in characteristic ways. Therefore, mental states are best understood as functional states.

The strongest objection to functionalism comes from the problem of qualia. Consider the inverted spectrum thought experiment: it seems conceivable that two people could be functionally identical — they respond to the same inputs with the same outputs, they use color words the same way — and yet one sees red where the other sees green. If this is possible, then there is something about conscious experience that functional role does not capture. The qualitative character of experience, what it is like to see red, seems to be left out of the functionalist picture.

The functionalist can respond in several ways. One strategy is to deny that inverted qualia are genuinely possible. If two people are truly functionally identical in every respect, including all their internal states and their relations to each other, then there is no room for a difference in qualitative experience. The apparent conceivability of inverted qualia may reflect a failure of imagination rather than a genuine metaphysical possibility. Another strategy is to argue that even if qualia exist, they are functionally definable at a sufficiently fine-grained level of description. The functionalist need not capture qualia at the level of simple input-output relations; a more detailed functional analysis that includes internal monitoring states and higher-order representations may succeed where a simpler analysis fails.

Nevertheless, the qualia objection retains force. Even the most detailed functional description seems to leave open the question of why these functional relations are accompanied by conscious experience at all. This is a version of the hard problem of consciousness, and functionalism, like all materialist theories, must ultimately confront it. However, the functionalist is better positioned than the identity theorist or the behaviorist, because functionalism at least provides a framework for asking precise questions about which functional properties are associated with consciousness and which are not. The question becomes tractable, even if it is not yet answered.
